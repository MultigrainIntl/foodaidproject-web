Technology & intelligence

Useful intelligence should not be reserved for the organizations with the largest technology budgets.

Food Aid Project’s technology work is about access: bringing weather, geospatial, crop, freight and market context into decisions that strengthen food and economic security.

From price and freight to a wider field of view.

The original software idea joined commodity prices with freight rates to show delivered cost quickly. The current development direction expands that logic: decisions improve when people can see more of the system.

GISit Weather

A live public weather gateway brings forecasts, alerts, radar, air quality and agricultural pathways into one interface.

Visit GISit Weather →

GISit Farm Pro

An in-development field decision-support direction focused on operational windows, risk, crop and soil context, and ground truth.

Crop intelligence

In-development capabilities explore crop conditions, field identification, yield context and the connection between production visibility and market access.

Procurement intelligence

The founding idea remains relevant: combine product, supplier and freight knowledge so nonprofit buyers can evaluate delivered cost more effectively.

Ownership and access

Technology with a clear boundary.

GISit LLC owns the technology. George Jibilian personally funds and develops it. Food Aid Project and its programs receive free, limited access for charitable purposes.

Some nonprofit funding may support future mission-related enhancements. Any such support should be documented, independently reviewed under Food Aid Project’s conflict policy and tied to public benefit.

Food Aid Project does not claim ownership of every GISit product, and development work is not presented as deployed impact.

Development principles

Practical first

Start with a decision someone actually needs to make.

Access by design

Create mission pathways for organizations with limited resources.

Evidence aware

Distinguish prototypes, pilots, public tools and validated outcomes.

Human in the loop

Technology supports judgment; it does not replace responsibility.
